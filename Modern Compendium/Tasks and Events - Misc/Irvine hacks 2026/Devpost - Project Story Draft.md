## Challenges we ran into

Our initial concept of utilizing the piezo electric sensors came from the inspiration of the earthquake detection and how earthquake epicenters are detected. By measuring the arrival times of waves across multiple sensors, its possible to triangulate the origin of the event. We wanted to apply this same principle to a pickleball paddle through the utilization of piezoelectric sensors to detect and localize hit placements based off precise timing data.

//Our first major challenge was signal reliability. The piezoelectric sensor produced very short, sharp voltage spikes that were difficult to detect consistently. Hits were only registered when the paddle was struck with excessive force, making the system unreliable for normal gameplay.



To improve sensitivity, we modified the sensor configuration by reading the piezoelectric element across a parallel resistor. This adjusted the charge and discharge behavior of the sensor, effectively shaping the signal into a cleaner, more defined edge suitable for interrupt-based detection. With this change, we were able to reliably trigger interrupts and timestamp hits without interfering with other sensor readings.

However, this improvement introduced a new issue. The voltage spikes generated by the piezoelectric sensor were large enough to overwhelm the Arduino’s analog input circuitry, occasionally causing soft resets that erased recorded timestamps.

To protect the microcontroller, we added a series resistor to the interrupt pin to limit current and prevent voltage spikes from damaging or destabilizing the board. This stabilized the system and allowed us to continue collecting accurate impact timing data.
